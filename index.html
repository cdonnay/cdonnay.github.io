About me

Chris(topher) Donnay is currently the lab manager for the Data and Democracy Lab at Cornell University. He got his PhD in mathematics from the Ohio State University under Matthew Kahle, co-advised by Moon Duchin. He studied the mathematics of redistricting and stochastic topology. Chris also holds a Master's of Science in Education from the University of Pennsylvania, where he was a teaching fellow in the Independent School Teaching Residency program.

Programming

Chris is the principal developer for VoteKit, a Python package for modeling and analyzing ranked-choice voting. As a teaching fellow at UPenn, he designed, wrote, and taught the curriculum for an Introduction to Python course. He has experience batching large experiments to high-performance clusters using SLURM.

Communication

Chris is passionate about science communciation. He has written numerous collections of teaching materials, Python tutorials, research papers, and white papers for community and advocacy groups. He has presented the Lab's research to a variety of audiences, including funders, academics, and community members.

Research

Chris' research interests broadly include the mathematics of democracy (gerrymandering, ranked-choice voting) , topology (stochastic, topological data analysis), and Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods. In his current role as lab manager, he focuses on community-driven research questions of the form, "How would switching to ranked-choice voting impact my community?".

Asymptotics of Redistricting

How many ways are there to redistricting an nxn grid into n districts? How does this grow with n? What can this tell us about the shape of a typical district?

Find out in "Asymptotics of Redistricting the $n\times n$ grid" by C. Donnay and M. Kahle, to appear in The American Mathematical Monthly, November 2025.

VoteKit

VoteKit is a one-stop shop for all things election modeling. With an end-to-end pipeline for constructing ballots, running elections, and analyzing outcomes, VoteKit seeks to be a tool for academics, researchers, and practitioners alike.

See "VoteKit: A Python package for computational social choice research." by C. Donnay, M. Duchin, J. Gibson, Z. Glaser, A. Hong, M. Mukundan, and J. Wang, Journal of Open Source Software, May 2025.

3:1 Nesting Rules in Redistricting

Some states, like Ohio, require that their senate districts be composed of house districts. How does this affect the space of feasible plans? How does this affect the ability of a map-maker to gerrymander?

Find out in "3:1 Nesting Rules in Redistricting," by C. Donnay, in revisions with Statistics and Public Policy.
